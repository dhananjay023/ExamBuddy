Chapter 9: Heredity and Evolution

Class 10 Science Updated: May 15, 2025

Introduction

Heredity and Evolution is a fascinating chapter in Class 10 Science that explores how traits are passed from parents to offspring and how species change over time. This chapter introduces fundamental concepts of genetics, inheritance patterns, and evolutionary processes that help us understand the diversity of life on Earth.

Key Concepts

Heredity and Inheritance

  • Heredity is the transmission of traits from parents to offspring through genes
  • Traits are characteristics that distinguish one individual from another
  • Genes are units of heredity that control specific traits
  • Chromosomes contain genes and are present in the nucleus of cells
  • DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid) is the hereditary material that carries genetic information
  • Genotype refers to the genetic makeup of an organism
  • Phenotype refers to the observable characteristics of an organism

Mendel's Laws of Inheritance

  • Gregor Mendel is known as the "Father of Genetics" for his work on pea plants
  • Law of Dominance: In a pair of contrasting traits, one trait (dominant) masks the expression of the other (recessive)
  • Law of Segregation: Each parent contributes one factor (allele) for each trait to the offspring
  • Law of Independent Assortment: Different traits are inherited independently of each other
  • Dominant traits are expressed with capital letters (A, B, T)
  • Recessive traits are expressed with small letters (a, b, t)
  • Homozygous: having identical alleles for a trait (AA or aa)
  • Heterozygous: having different alleles for a trait (Aa)

Monohybrid and Dihybrid Crosses

  • Monohybrid cross involves the study of inheritance of a single trait
  • In F1 generation of monohybrid cross, all offspring show dominant trait
  • In F2 generation, the ratio is 3:1 (dominant : recessive)
  • Dihybrid cross involves the study of inheritance of two traits simultaneously
  • In F2 generation of dihybrid cross, the ratio is 9:3:3:1
  • Test cross is performed to determine the genotype of an organism showing dominant phenotype
  • Punnett square is a tool used to predict the outcome of genetic crosses

Monohybrid Cross Ratio

F2 Generation: 3:1 (Dominant : Recessive)

Genotypic ratio: 1:2:1 (AA : Aa : aa)

Dihybrid Cross Ratio

F2 Generation: 9:3:3:1

9 (both dominant) : 3 (first dominant) : 3 (second dominant) : 1 (both recessive)

Sex Determination

  • Sex chromosomes determine the sex of an individual
  • In humans, females have XX chromosomes and males have XY chromosomes
  • The male parent determines the sex of the offspring
  • Sex ratio in humans is approximately 1:1 (male : female)
  • Sex-linked traits are controlled by genes present on sex chromosomes
  • X-linked traits are more common in males as they have only one X chromosome
  • Examples of sex-linked disorders: Color blindness, Hemophilia

Sex Determination

Male (XY) × Female (XX)

Offspring ratio: 50% Male (XY) : 50% Female (XX)

Evolution

  • Evolution is the gradual change in species over time
  • Charles Darwin proposed the theory of evolution by natural selection
  • Natural selection is the process by which organisms with favorable traits survive and reproduce
  • Variation is the raw material for evolution
  • Sources of variation include genetic recombination, mutations, and environmental factors
  • Survival of the fittest means organisms best adapted to their environment survive
  • Speciation is the formation of new species from existing ones
  • Reproductive isolation leads to speciation

Evidence for Evolution

  • Fossil records provide evidence of past life forms and their changes over time
  • Comparative anatomy shows similarities in bone structure among different species
  • Homologous organs have the same basic structure but different functions
  • Analogous organs have different structures but similar functions
  • Vestigial organs are remnants of organs that were functional in ancestors
  • Embryological evidence shows similarities in early developmental stages
  • Molecular evidence includes similarities in DNA and protein sequences
  • Biogeographical evidence shows distribution patterns of species

Human Evolution

  • Humans evolved from early primates over millions of years
  • Key stages include Dryopithecus, Ramapithecus, Australopithecus, Homo habilis, Homo erectus, and Homo sapiens
  • Important evolutionary changes include bipedalism, increased brain size, and tool use
  • Homo sapiens (modern humans) appeared around 200,000 years ago
  • All modern humans belong to the same species despite variations in appearance
  • Human races are social constructs, not biological realities
  • Genetic diversity within populations is greater than between populations

Important Questions and Solutions

Question 1

What is the difference between homologous and analogous organs? Give examples.

Solution:

Homologous Organs:

  • Have the same basic structure and origin
  • May have different functions
  • Indicate common ancestry
  • Examples: Forelimbs of mammals (human hand, bat wing, whale flipper, horse leg)

Analogous Organs:

  • Have different structures and origins
  • Perform similar functions
  • Result of convergent evolution
  • Examples: Wings of birds and insects, eyes of vertebrates and cephalopods

Homologous organs provide evidence for divergent evolution, while analogous organs provide evidence for convergent evolution.

Question 2

In a monohybrid cross between tall (TT) and dwarf (tt) pea plants, what will be the genotype and phenotype of F1 and F2 generations?

Solution:

Parent Cross: TT (Tall) × tt (Dwarf)

F1 Generation:

Genotype: All Tt (100%)

Phenotype: All Tall (100%)

F2 Generation (F1 × F1: Tt × Tt):

Genotype: 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt (1:2:1 ratio)

Phenotype: 3 Tall : 1 Dwarf (3:1 ratio)

Punnett Square for F2:

    T    t
T  TT   Tt
t  Tt   tt

This demonstrates Mendel's Law of Dominance and Law of Segregation.

Question 3

Explain how sex is determined in human beings. Why is the male parent responsible for determining the sex of the child?

Solution:

Sex Determination in Humans:

Sex is determined by sex chromosomes:

  • Females have XX chromosomes (homogametic)
  • Males have XY chromosomes (heterogametic)

Process:

Female (XX) can only contribute X chromosome to offspring

Male (XY) can contribute either X or Y chromosome

Cross:

       X    X
   X  XX   XX  (Female)
   Y  XY   XY  (Male)

Why male parent determines sex:

Since the female can only give X chromosome, the sex of the child depends on whether the male contributes X (resulting in female XX) or Y (resulting in male XY) chromosome. Therefore, the male parent is responsible for determining the sex of the child.

Result: 50% chance of male and 50% chance of female offspring.

Common Misconceptions

Dominant vs. Common

Misconception: Dominant traits are more common in a population.

Correction: Dominance refers to gene expression, not frequency. A recessive trait can be more common than a dominant trait in a population depending on allele frequencies.

Evolution as "Just a Theory"

Misconception: Evolution is "just a theory" and therefore uncertain.

Correction: In science, a theory is a well-substantiated explanation supported by extensive evidence. Evolution is both a fact (organisms change over time) and a theory (natural selection explains how).

Inheritance of Acquired Characteristics

Misconception: Traits acquired during an organism's lifetime can be passed to offspring.

Correction: Only genetic traits are inherited. Acquired characteristics (like muscle development from exercise) are not passed on genetically, though epigenetic factors may play a role.

NCERT Exercise Solutions

Below are the solutions to selected questions from the NCERT exercise.

Exercise Question 1

A cross between a tall plant (TT) and short plant (tt) resulted in progeny that were all tall plants because:

(a) Tallness is the dominant trait

(b) Shortness is the dominant trait

(c) Tallness is the recessive trait

(d) Height of plant is not governed by gene/factor

Solution:

Answer: (a) Tallness is the dominant trait

Explanation:

When a homozygous tall plant (TT) is crossed with a homozygous short plant (tt), all F1 offspring have the genotype Tt.

Since all F1 plants show the tall phenotype, this indicates that the allele for tallness (T) is dominant over the allele for shortness (t).

According to Mendel's Law of Dominance, in a heterozygous condition (Tt), the dominant trait (tallness) is expressed while the recessive trait (shortness) is masked.

Cross: TT × tt → All Tt (tall phenotype)
