Chapter 1: Chemical Reactions and Equations

Class 10 Science Updated: May 15, 2025

Introduction

Chemical Reactions and Equations is the foundational chapter of Class 10 Science. It introduces students to the world of chemical changes, helping them understand how substances interact and transform into new products. This chapter covers the fundamental concepts of chemical reactions, their types, and how to represent them using chemical equations.

Key Concepts

Chemical Reactions

  • A chemical reaction is a process in which one or more substances (reactants) are converted into one or more different substances (products)
  • Chemical reactions involve breaking of old bonds and formation of new bonds
  • During a chemical reaction, the composition of substances changes
  • Chemical reactions are accompanied by energy changes (absorption or release of heat, light, etc.)
  • Examples: Burning of paper, rusting of iron, digestion of food

Chemical Equations

  • A chemical equation is a symbolic representation of a chemical reaction
  • Reactants are written on the left side and products on the right side
  • An arrow (→) separates reactants from products
  • Chemical equations must be balanced to follow the law of conservation of mass
  • Example: Mg + O₂ → MgO (unbalanced), 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO (balanced)

Balancing Chemical Equations

  • Balancing ensures that the number of atoms of each element is equal on both sides
  • Only coefficients (numbers in front of formulas) can be changed, not subscripts
  • Start balancing with the most complex molecule
  • Balance metals first, then non-metals, and finally hydrogen and oxygen
  • Check that the equation is balanced by counting atoms of each element

Steps for Balancing

1. Write the unbalanced equation

2. Count atoms of each element

3. Add coefficients to balance

4. Verify the balance

Types of Chemical Reactions

  • Combination Reaction: Two or more reactants combine to form a single product
    • A + B → AB
    • Example: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
  • Decomposition Reaction: A single reactant breaks down into two or more products
    • AB → A + B
    • Example: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂ (electrolysis)
  • Displacement Reaction: A more reactive element displaces a less reactive element
    • A + BC → AC + B
    • Example: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu
  • Double Displacement Reaction: Exchange of ions between two compounds
    • AB + CD → AD + CB
    • Example: AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl + NaNO₃

Oxidation and Reduction

  • Oxidation: Addition of oxygen or removal of hydrogen from a substance
  • Reduction: Addition of hydrogen or removal of oxygen from a substance
  • Oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously in a reaction (redox reaction)
  • The substance that gets oxidized is called a reducing agent
  • The substance that gets reduced is called an oxidizing agent
  • Example: CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O (CuO is reduced, H₂ is oxidized)

Redox Reaction

Oxidation: Loss of electrons (OIL)
Reduction: Gain of electrons (RIG)

Effects of Oxidation in Daily Life

  • Corrosion: Oxidation of metals in the presence of air and moisture
    • Rusting of iron: 4Fe + 3O₂ + 6H₂O → 2Fe₂O₃·3H₂O
    • Prevention: Painting, galvanization, alloying
  • Rancidity: Oxidation of fats and oils in food
    • Causes unpleasant smell and taste
    • Prevention: Adding antioxidants, proper storage, refrigeration

Important Questions and Solutions

Question 1

Balance the following chemical equation: Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂

Solution:

Step 1: Write the unbalanced equation

Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂

Step 2: Count atoms on both sides

Left side: Fe = 1, H = 2, O = 1

Right side: Fe = 3, H = 2, O = 4

Step 3: Balance Fe atoms first

3Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂

Step 4: Balance O atoms

3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂

Step 5: Balance H atoms

3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂

Balanced equation: 3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂

Question 2

Identify the type of reaction and write a balanced equation for: Calcium carbonate decomposes on heating to give calcium oxide and carbon dioxide.

Solution:

Type of reaction: Decomposition reaction

Reason: A single compound (calcium carbonate) breaks down into two simpler compounds (calcium oxide and carbon dioxide).

Unbalanced equation: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂

Balanced equation: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂

(This equation is already balanced as there is 1 Ca, 1 C, and 3 O atoms on both sides)

Question 3

What happens when zinc reacts with copper sulfate solution? Write the chemical equation and identify the type of reaction.

Solution:

Observation: When zinc is added to copper sulfate solution, the blue color of the solution fades and reddish-brown copper metal is deposited on the zinc.

Chemical equation: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu

Type of reaction: Displacement reaction (Single displacement)

Explanation: Zinc is more reactive than copper, so it displaces copper from copper sulfate solution. The zinc gets oxidized to Zn²⁺ ions, while Cu²⁺ ions get reduced to metallic copper.

Key Formulas and Concepts

Combination Reaction

A + B → AB

Decomposition Reaction

AB → A + B

Displacement Reaction

A + BC → AC + B

Double Displacement

AB + CD → AD + CB

Law of Conservation of Mass

Mass of reactants = Mass of products

4Fe + 3O₂ + 6H₂O → 2Fe₂O₃·3H₂O

Common Misconceptions

Balancing Equations

Misconception: Subscripts in chemical formulas can be changed to balance equations.

Correction: Only coefficients (numbers in front of formulas) can be changed. Changing subscripts changes the identity of the compound.

Physical vs Chemical Changes

Misconception: All changes involving heat are chemical reactions.

Correction: Melting ice or boiling water involve heat but are physical changes. Chemical reactions involve formation of new substances with different properties.

Oxidation Definition

Misconception: Oxidation always involves oxygen.

Correction: While historically defined as reaction with oxygen, oxidation is more accurately defined as loss of electrons or increase in oxidation state.

NCERT Exercise Solutions

Below are the solutions to selected questions from the NCERT exercise.

Exercise Question 1

Which of the statements about the reaction below are incorrect?
2PbO(s) + C(s) → 2Pb(s) + CO₂(g)
(a) Lead is getting reduced
(b) Carbon dioxide is getting oxidized
(c) Carbon is getting oxidized
(d) Lead oxide is getting reduced

Solution:

Analysis of the reaction: 2PbO(s) + C(s) → 2Pb(s) + CO₂(g)

(a) Lead is getting reduced - CORRECT
Lead in PbO has oxidation state +2, and in Pb it becomes 0. Since oxidation state decreases, lead is getting reduced.

(b) Carbon dioxide is getting oxidized - INCORRECT
CO₂ is a product, not a reactant. Products don't get oxidized or reduced in the reaction; they are formed as a result of the reaction.

(c) Carbon is getting oxidized - CORRECT
Carbon has oxidation state 0 in C(s) and +4 in CO₂. Since oxidation state increases, carbon is getting oxidized.

(d) Lead oxide is getting reduced - CORRECT
PbO loses oxygen to form Pb, which means it's getting reduced.

Answer: Statement (b) is incorrect.
