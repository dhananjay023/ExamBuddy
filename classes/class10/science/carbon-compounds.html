Chapter 4: Carbon and its Compounds

Class 10 Science Updated: May 15, 2025

Introduction

Carbon and its Compounds is one of the most important chapters in Class 10 Science. Carbon forms the backbone of all organic molecules and is essential for life on Earth. This chapter introduces students to the unique properties of carbon, various types of carbon compounds, their nomenclature, properties, and reactions. Understanding carbon compounds is crucial as they form the basis of biochemistry and many industrial processes.

Key Concepts

Unique Properties of Carbon

  • Carbon has atomic number 6 with electronic configuration 2, 4
  • It has 4 valence electrons, allowing it to form 4 covalent bonds
  • Carbon shows tetravalency (valency of 4)
  • It can form single, double, and triple bonds with other carbon atoms
  • Carbon exhibits catenation - the ability to form chains with other carbon atoms
  • It can form straight chains, branched chains, and ring structures
  • Carbon forms stable covalent bonds due to its small size
  • It can bond with hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, sulfur, and halogens

Allotropes of Carbon

  • Allotropes are different structural forms of the same element
  • Diamond: Each carbon is bonded to 4 other carbons in tetrahedral arrangement
  • Diamond is the hardest natural substance and has high melting point
  • It is a poor conductor of electricity but good conductor of heat
  • Graphite: Each carbon is bonded to 3 other carbons in hexagonal layers
  • Graphite is soft, slippery, and conducts electricity
  • It is used in pencils, electrodes, and as lubricant
  • Fullerenes: Spherical carbon structures like C₆₀ (Buckminsterfullerene)

Hydrocarbons

  • Hydrocarbons are compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen
  • Saturated Hydrocarbons (Alkanes): Contain only single bonds
  • General formula for alkanes: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂
  • Examples: Methane (CH₄), Ethane (C₂H₆), Propane (C₃H₈), Butane (C₄H₁₀)
  • Unsaturated Hydrocarbons: Contain double or triple bonds
  • Alkenes: Contain double bonds, general formula CₙH₂ₙ
  • Examples: Ethene (C₂H₄), Propene (C₃H₆)
  • Alkynes: Contain triple bonds, general formula CₙH₂ₙ₋₂
  • Examples: Ethyne (C₂H₂), Propyne (C₃H₄)

Hydrocarbon Formulas

Alkanes: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂
Alkenes: CₙH₂ₙ
Alkynes: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂

Where n is the number of carbon atoms

Functional Groups

  • Functional groups are atoms or groups of atoms that determine chemical properties
  • Alcohols (-OH): Methanol (CH₃OH), Ethanol (C₂H₅OH)
  • Aldehydes (-CHO): Methanal (HCHO), Ethanal (CH₃CHO)
  • Ketones (>CO): Propanone (CH₃COCH₃)
  • Carboxylic acids (-COOH): Methanoic acid (HCOOH), Ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH)
  • Esters (-COO-): Formed by reaction of acids with alcohols
  • Functional groups undergo characteristic reactions
  • They determine the chemical behavior of organic compounds

Nomenclature of Carbon Compounds

  • IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) system is used
  • Find the longest carbon chain (parent chain)
  • Number the carbon atoms from the end closest to functional group
  • Identify and name the functional group
  • Name substituents (branches) with their positions
  • Alkane names: Meth-, Eth-, Prop-, But-, Pent-, Hex-
  • Functional group suffixes: -ol (alcohol), -al (aldehyde), -one (ketone), -oic acid (carboxylic acid)
  • Use prefixes like di-, tri-, tetra- for multiple identical groups

Chemical Properties of Carbon Compounds

  • Combustion: Carbon compounds burn in air to form CO₂ and H₂O
  • Complete combustion produces CO₂, incomplete combustion produces CO
  • Oxidation: Alcohols can be oxidized to aldehydes and then to carboxylic acids
  • Addition reactions: Unsaturated compounds add hydrogen, halogens, etc.
  • Substitution reactions: One atom/group is replaced by another
  • Saturated compounds undergo substitution reactions
  • Unsaturated compounds undergo addition reactions
  • Esterification: Acids react with alcohols to form esters

Important Reactions

CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O (Combustion)
C₂H₄ + H₂ → C₂H₆ (Addition)
CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl (Substitution)

Important Carbon Compounds in Daily Life

  • Ethanol (C₂H₅OH): Used in alcoholic beverages, as solvent, antiseptic
  • Prepared by fermentation of sugars or hydration of ethene
  • Ethanoic Acid (CH₃COOH): Main component of vinegar
  • Used in food preservation and manufacturing
  • Soaps and Detergents: Made from long-chain carboxylic acids
  • Soaps are sodium or potassium salts of fatty acids
  • Detergents are synthetic cleaning agents
  • Esters: Used in perfumes, flavoring agents, and as solvents

Important Questions and Solutions

Question 1

Explain why carbon forms a large number of compounds. What is catenation?

Solution:

Carbon forms a large number of compounds due to the following reasons:

  1. Tetravalency: Carbon has 4 valence electrons, allowing it to form 4 covalent bonds with other atoms
  2. Catenation: Carbon can form stable bonds with other carbon atoms, creating chains and rings
  3. Small size: Carbon's small atomic size allows for effective orbital overlap, forming strong covalent bonds
  4. Multiple bonding: Carbon can form single, double, and triple bonds
  5. Isomerism: Same molecular formula can have different structural arrangements

Catenation:

Catenation is the ability of carbon atoms to form bonds with other carbon atoms, creating long chains, branched structures, and ring compounds. This property is unique to carbon among all elements and is responsible for the existence of millions of organic compounds.

Examples of catenation:

  • Straight chains: C-C-C-C
  • Branched chains: C-C-C(C)-C
  • Ring structures: Benzene, cyclohexane

Question 2

What are saturated and unsaturated hydrocarbons? Give examples and write their general formulas.

Solution:

Saturated Hydrocarbons (Alkanes):

These are hydrocarbons in which carbon atoms are connected by only single bonds. They are called "saturated" because they contain the maximum number of hydrogen atoms possible.

General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂

Examples:

  • Methane: CH₄
  • Ethane: C₂H₆
  • Propane: C₃H₈
  • Butane: C₄H₁₀

Unsaturated Hydrocarbons:

These are hydrocarbons that contain double or triple bonds between carbon atoms. They are called "unsaturated" because they can add more hydrogen atoms.

Alkenes (Double bonds):

General formula: CₙH₂ₙ

Examples: Ethene (C₂H₄), Propene (C₃H₆)

Alkynes (Triple bonds):

General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂

Examples: Ethyne (C₂H₂), Propyne (C₃H₄)

Question 3

Explain the cleaning action of soap. Why do soaps not work well in hard water?

Solution:

Cleaning Action of Soap:

Soap molecules have two parts:

  1. Hydrophilic head: Water-loving ionic part (COO⁻Na⁺)
  2. Hydrophobic tail: Water-hating hydrocarbon chain

Mechanism:

  1. When soap is added to water, it forms micelles (spherical structures)
  2. The hydrophobic tails point inward, hydrophilic heads point outward
  3. Oil and grease get trapped inside the hydrophobic core of micelles
  4. The micelles remain suspended in water due to hydrophilic heads
  5. Dirt and oil are washed away with water

Why soaps don't work in hard water:

Hard water contains dissolved salts of calcium and magnesium (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺). These ions react with soap to form insoluble precipitates:

2C₁₇H₃₅COONa + Ca²⁺ → (C₁₇H₃₅COO)₂Ca↓ + 2Na⁺

Problems caused:

  • Formation of scum (insoluble precipitate)
  • Wastage of soap
  • Reduced cleaning efficiency
  • Clothes become stiff and rough

Solution: Use detergents instead, as they don't form precipitates with hard water.

Common Misconceptions

All Carbon Compounds are Organic

Misconception: All compounds containing carbon are organic compounds.

Correction: Some carbon compounds like carbon dioxide (CO₂), carbon monoxide (CO), carbonates, and bicarbonates are considered inorganic compounds because they don't contain carbon-hydrogen bonds and don't show typical organic properties.

Diamond and Graphite are Different Elements

Misconception: Diamond and graphite are different elements because they have different properties.

Correction: Diamond and graphite are allotropes of the same element (carbon). They have different properties due to different arrangements of carbon atoms, not because they are different elements.

Unsaturated Compounds are Less Stable

Misconception: Unsaturated compounds are always less stable than saturated compounds.

Correction: While unsaturated compounds are generally more reactive due to multiple bonds, some unsaturated compounds like benzene are highly stable due to resonance and aromaticity.

NCERT Exercise Solutions

Below are the solutions to selected questions from the NCERT exercise.

Exercise Question 1

What would be the electron dot structure of carbon dioxide which has the formula CO₂?

Solution:

Step-by-step approach:

  1. Identify atoms: 1 carbon atom and 2 oxygen atoms
  2. Count valence electrons:
    • Carbon: 4 valence electrons
    • Each oxygen: 6 valence electrons
    • Total: 4 + 6 + 6 = 16 valence electrons
  3. Arrange atoms: Carbon is the central atom (less electronegative)
  4. Form bonds: Carbon forms double bonds with each oxygen

Electron dot structure:

O :: C :: O

Where :: represents a double bond (4 shared electrons)

Explanation:

  • Carbon shares 4 electrons (2 with each oxygen)
  • Each oxygen shares 4 electrons with carbon
  • Each oxygen has 4 unshared electrons (2 lone pairs)
  • All atoms achieve stable octet configuration
