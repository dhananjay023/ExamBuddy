Chapter 6: Life Processes

Class 10 Science Updated: May 15, 2025

Introduction

Life Processes is a fundamental chapter in Class 10 Science that explores the various processes that are essential for maintaining life in organisms. This chapter covers nutrition, respiration, transportation, and excretion - the four major life processes that keep living organisms alive and functioning.

Key Concepts

Nutrition

  • Nutrition is the process by which organisms obtain and utilize food for energy, growth, and maintenance
  • There are two main types of nutrition: autotrophic and heterotrophic
  • Autotrophic nutrition: Organisms make their own food (e.g., plants through photosynthesis)
  • Heterotrophic nutrition: Organisms depend on other organisms for food (e.g., animals, fungi)
  • Photosynthesis occurs in chloroplasts and requires sunlight, carbon dioxide, and water
  • The equation for photosynthesis: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
  • Human digestive system includes mouth, esophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine, and associated organs

Respiration

  • Respiration is the process of breaking down food molecules to release energy
  • There are two types: aerobic respiration (with oxygen) and anaerobic respiration (without oxygen)
  • Aerobic respiration: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + 38 ATP
  • Anaerobic respiration in plants: C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂ + 2 ATP (alcoholic fermentation)
  • Anaerobic respiration in animals: C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₃H₆O₃ + 2 ATP (lactic acid fermentation)
  • Human respiratory system includes nasal cavity, trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, and alveoli
  • Gas exchange occurs in alveoli through diffusion

Transportation

  • Transportation is the movement of substances like nutrients, gases, and waste products within organisms
  • In plants, transportation occurs through xylem and phloem tissues
  • Xylem transports water and minerals from roots to leaves
  • Phloem transports food (glucose) from leaves to other parts of the plant
  • Transpiration helps in the upward movement of water in plants
  • Human circulatory system consists of heart, blood vessels, and blood
  • Heart has four chambers: two atria and two ventricles
  • Blood carries oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and waste products
  • Double circulation: pulmonary circulation and systemic circulation

Excretion

  • Excretion is the removal of harmful metabolic wastes from the body
  • In plants, waste products are stored in vacuoles or removed through stomata
  • Plants also excrete oxygen during photosynthesis and carbon dioxide during respiration
  • Human excretory system includes kidneys, ureters, bladder, and urethra
  • Kidneys filter blood and form urine through three processes: filtration, reabsorption, and secretion
  • Nephron is the functional unit of the kidney
  • Other excretory organs: lungs (CO₂), skin (sweat), and liver (bile pigments)

Important Questions and Solutions

Question 1

What is photosynthesis? Write the balanced chemical equation for photosynthesis and explain the process.

Solution:

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants and some bacteria convert light energy (usually from the sun) into chemical energy in the form of glucose, using carbon dioxide and water.

Balanced chemical equation:

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

Process of photosynthesis:

  1. Light absorption: Chlorophyll in chloroplasts absorbs light energy
  2. Water splitting: Water molecules are split into hydrogen and oxygen
  3. Carbon fixation: Carbon dioxide is fixed into organic compounds
  4. Glucose formation: Simple sugars are formed and can be converted to glucose
  5. Oxygen release: Oxygen is released as a byproduct

Question 2

Differentiate between aerobic and anaerobic respiration. Give examples of each.

Solution:

Aerobic Respiration Anaerobic Respiration
Occurs in the presence of oxygen Occurs in the absence of oxygen
Produces 38 ATP molecules Produces only 2 ATP molecules
End products: CO₂ and H₂O End products: Ethanol/Lactic acid and CO₂
Complete oxidation of glucose Incomplete oxidation of glucose

Examples:

Aerobic: Most animals, including humans, during normal activities

Anaerobic: Yeast fermentation (alcoholic), muscle cells during intense exercise (lactic acid)

Question 3

Describe the structure and function of nephron in the human excretory system.

Solution:

The nephron is the basic functional unit of the kidney. Each kidney contains about 1 million nephrons.

Structure of Nephron:

  1. Bowman's capsule: Cup-shaped structure that surrounds the glomerulus
  2. Glomerulus: Cluster of tiny blood capillaries
  3. Proximal convoluted tubule: First coiled part of the tubule
  4. Loop of Henle: U-shaped portion with descending and ascending limbs
  5. Distal convoluted tubule: Second coiled part of the tubule
  6. Collecting duct: Final part that collects urine from multiple nephrons

Functions:

  1. Filtration: Blood is filtered in the glomerulus, and filtrate enters Bowman's capsule
  2. Reabsorption: Useful substances like glucose, amino acids, and water are reabsorbed
  3. Secretion: Additional waste products are secreted into the tubule
  4. Concentration: Final concentration of urine occurs in the collecting duct

Key Equations

Photosynthesis

Aerobic Respiration

Common Misconceptions

Plants Don't Respire

Misconception: Plants only carry out photosynthesis and don't respire.

Correction: Plants carry out both photosynthesis and respiration. Photosynthesis occurs only in the presence of light, while respiration occurs continuously, both day and night.

Breathing and Respiration Are the Same

Misconception: Breathing and respiration are the same process.

Correction: Breathing is the physical process of inhaling and exhaling air, while respiration is the biochemical process of energy release from food molecules.

Excretion Only Through Kidneys

Misconception: Excretion in humans occurs only through kidneys.

Correction: Excretion occurs through multiple organs: kidneys (urea), lungs (CO₂), skin (sweat), and liver (bile pigments).

NCERT Exercise Solutions

Below are the solutions to selected questions from the NCERT exercise.

Exercise Question 1

Why is diffusion insufficient to meet the oxygen requirements of multicellular organisms like humans?

Solution:

Diffusion is insufficient to meet the oxygen requirements of multicellular organisms like humans due to the following reasons:

  1. Size and complexity: Multicellular organisms have a large body size with billions of cells arranged in multiple layers. Oxygen cannot reach the inner cells effectively through simple diffusion.
  2. Distance factor: In large organisms, the distance between the body surface and inner cells is very large. Diffusion is effective only over short distances (typically less than 1mm).
  3. Rate of diffusion: The rate of diffusion is very slow and would not be able to supply oxygen at the rate required by active metabolic processes in large organisms.
  4. High oxygen demand: Multicellular organisms have high metabolic rates and require large amounts of oxygen continuously, which cannot be met by diffusion alone.
  5. Surface area to volume ratio: As organisms increase in size, their surface area to volume ratio decreases, making diffusion even less efficient.

Therefore, multicellular organisms have evolved specialized respiratory and circulatory systems to efficiently transport oxygen to all cells and remove carbon dioxide.
