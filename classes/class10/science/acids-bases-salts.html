Chapter 2: Acids, Bases and Salts

Class 10 Science Updated: May 15, 2025

Introduction

Acids, Bases and Salts is a crucial chapter that introduces students to important chemical substances found in our daily lives. From the citric acid in lemons to the sodium hydroxide in soap, this chapter helps understand the properties, reactions, and applications of these fundamental chemical compounds.

Key Concepts

Acids

  • Acids are substances that release hydrogen ions (H⁺) when dissolved in water
  • Taste sour (though tasting acids is dangerous and should never be done)
  • Turn blue litmus paper red
  • React with metals to produce hydrogen gas
  • React with metal carbonates to produce carbon dioxide
  • Conduct electricity due to presence of ions
  • Examples: HCl (hydrochloric acid), H₂SO₄ (sulfuric acid), HNO₃ (nitric acid)

Bases

  • Bases are substances that release hydroxide ions (OH⁻) when dissolved in water
  • Taste bitter and feel soapy to touch
  • Turn red litmus paper blue
  • React with acids to form salt and water (neutralization)
  • Conduct electricity due to presence of ions
  • Examples: NaOH (sodium hydroxide), KOH (potassium hydroxide), Ca(OH)₂ (calcium hydroxide)
  • Alkalis are bases that are soluble in water

pH Scale

  • pH stands for 'potential of Hydrogen' or 'power of Hydrogen'
  • pH scale ranges from 0 to 14
  • pH = 7 indicates neutral solution (pure water)
  • pH < 7 indicates acidic solution
  • pH > 7 indicates basic/alkaline solution
  • Lower the pH value, stronger the acid
  • Higher the pH value, stronger the base
  • Each unit change represents 10-fold change in acidity

pH Scale

0 ← Strongly Acidic | 7 Neutral | Strongly Basic → 14

Examples: Lemon juice (pH ~2), Blood (pH ~7.4), Soap (pH ~9)

Indicators

  • Substances that show different colors in acidic and basic solutions
  • Natural Indicators:
    • Litmus: Red in acid, blue in base
    • Red cabbage extract: Red in acid, green in base
    • Turmeric: Yellow in acid, red in base
  • Synthetic Indicators:
    • Methyl orange: Red in acid, yellow in base
    • Phenolphthalein: Colorless in acid, pink in base
  • Universal Indicator: Shows different colors for different pH values

Neutralization Reaction

  • Reaction between an acid and a base to form salt and water
  • General equation: Acid + Base → Salt + Water
  • The reaction is exothermic (releases heat)
  • pH of the resulting solution depends on the strength of acid and base
  • Examples:
    • HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
    • H₂SO₄ + 2KOH → K₂SO₄ + 2H₂O

Neutralization

Acid + Base → Salt + Water

Salts

  • Salts are ionic compounds formed by neutralization of acids and bases
  • Generally have high melting and boiling points
  • Conduct electricity in molten state or aqueous solution
  • Types of Salts:
    • Normal salts: Complete neutralization (NaCl, K₂SO₄)
    • Acid salts: Partial neutralization of polybasic acid (NaHSO₄)
    • Basic salts: Partial neutralization of polyacidic base (Mg(OH)Cl)
  • pH of salt solutions:
    • Strong acid + Strong base = Neutral salt (pH = 7)
    • Strong acid + Weak base = Acidic salt (pH < 7)
    • Weak acid + Strong base = Basic salt (pH > 7)

Important Compounds

  • Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH):
    • Common name: Caustic soda
    • Used in soap and paper making, petroleum refining
    • Highly corrosive
  • Bleaching Powder (CaOCl₂):
    • Prepared by: Ca(OH)₂ + Cl₂ → CaOCl₂ + H₂O
    • Used for bleaching and disinfection
  • Baking Soda (NaHCO₃):
    • Sodium hydrogen carbonate
    • Used in baking, antacid, fire extinguisher
    • Decomposes on heating: 2NaHCO₃ → Na₂CO₃ + CO₂ + H₂O
  • Washing Soda (Na₂CO₃·10H₂O):
    • Used in glass, soap, and paper industries
    • Water softening agent
  • Plaster of Paris (CaSO₄·½H₂O):
    • Prepared by heating gypsum to 373K
    • Used for making casts and molds
    • Sets by absorbing water: CaSO₄·½H₂O + 1½H₂O → CaSO₄·2H₂O

Important Questions and Solutions

Question 1

A solution has a pH of 3. Is it acidic or basic? If the pH is changed to 11, how does the nature of the solution change?

Solution:

For pH = 3:

Since pH < 7, the solution is acidic in nature.

pH = 3 indicates that the solution is quite acidic (stronger than coffee or orange juice).

For pH = 11:

Since pH > 7, the solution becomes basic/alkaline in nature.

pH = 11 indicates that the solution is quite basic (similar to ammonia solution).

Change in nature:

The solution changes from acidic to basic nature. The change from pH 3 to pH 11 represents an increase in pH by 8 units, which means the solution becomes 10⁸ = 100,000,000 times less acidic (or more basic).

Question 2

Write the chemical equation for the preparation of bleaching powder and mention two of its uses.

Solution:

Preparation of Bleaching Powder:

Bleaching powder is prepared by passing chlorine gas over dry slaked lime (calcium hydroxide):

Chemical equation: Ca(OH)₂ + Cl₂ → CaOCl₂ + H₂O

Word equation: Calcium hydroxide + Chlorine → Calcium oxychloride + Water

Two uses of bleaching powder:

  1. Bleaching: Used for bleaching cotton and linen in textile industry, and for bleaching wood pulp in paper industry
  2. Disinfectant: Used as a disinfectant for water treatment in swimming pools and drinking water supply

Question 3

What happens when zinc reacts with hydrochloric acid? Write the chemical equation and identify the type of reaction.

Solution:

Observation: When zinc reacts with hydrochloric acid, hydrogen gas is evolved with effervescence (bubbling), and zinc chloride is formed.

Chemical equation: Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑

Type of reaction: This is a displacement reaction (also called single displacement reaction) where zinc displaces hydrogen from hydrochloric acid.

Test for hydrogen gas: The gas evolved can be tested by bringing a burning matchstick near it. Hydrogen burns with a 'pop' sound.

General reaction: Metal + Acid → Salt + Hydrogen gas

Common Misconceptions

All Bases are Alkalis

Misconception: All bases are alkalis and vice versa.

Correction: All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis. Alkalis are bases that are soluble in water. For example, Cu(OH)₂ is a base but not an alkali as it's insoluble in water.

pH Scale Limits

Misconception: pH values cannot go below 0 or above 14.

Correction: While the common pH scale is 0-14, very concentrated solutions can have pH values outside this range. The 0-14 scale is based on water at 25°C.

Neutralization Always Gives pH 7

Misconception: All neutralization reactions result in a solution with pH 7.

Correction: Only strong acid + strong base gives pH 7. Weak acid + strong base gives pH > 7, and strong acid + weak base gives pH < 7.

Exercise Question 1

You have two solutions A and B. The pH of solution A is 6 and pH of solution B is 8. Which solution has more hydrogen ion concentration? Also identify the nature of these solutions.

Solution:

Comparison of hydrogen ion concentration:

Solution A has pH = 6, Solution B has pH = 8

Lower pH means higher concentration of H⁺ ions.

Since pH of A < pH of B, solution A has more hydrogen ion concentration than solution B.

Nature of solutions:

Solution A (pH = 6): Since pH < 7, it is acidic in nature

Solution B (pH = 8): Since pH > 7, it is basic/alkaline in nature

Quantitative difference:

Solution A has 10²⁻⁶ M H⁺ ions

Solution B has 10²⁻⁸ M H⁺ ions

Therefore, solution A has 100 times more H⁺ ions than solution B.
