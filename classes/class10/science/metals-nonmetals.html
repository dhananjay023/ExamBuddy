Chapter 3: Metals and Non-metals

Class 10 Science Updated: May 15, 2025

Introduction

Metals and non-metals are two major categories of elements that exhibit distinct physical and chemical properties. This chapter explores the properties of metals and non-metals, their occurrence in nature, extraction methods, and various applications in everyday life.

Key Concepts

Physical Properties of Metals

  • Metallic lustre: Metals have a shiny appearance when freshly cut
  • Malleability: Metals can be hammered into thin sheets
  • Ductility: Metals can be drawn into thin wires
  • Good conductors of heat and electricity
  • High density (except alkali metals like sodium and potassium)
  • High melting and boiling points (except mercury and alkali metals)
  • Produce metallic sound when struck (sonorous)
  • Generally hard (except alkali metals)

Physical Properties of Non-metals

  • Dull appearance (no metallic lustre), except iodine and graphite
  • Brittle in solid state (break into pieces when hammered)
  • Non-malleable and non-ductile
  • Poor conductors of heat and electricity (except graphite)
  • Low density
  • Low melting and boiling points (except diamond and graphite)
  • Do not produce sound when struck
  • Generally soft (except diamond)

Chemical Properties of Metals

  • Reaction with oxygen: Most metals form metal oxides which are basic in nature
    • Metal + Oxygen → Metal oxide
    • Example: 4Na + O₂ → 2Na₂O
  • Reaction with water: Some metals react with water to form metal hydroxides and hydrogen gas
    • Metal + Water → Metal hydroxide + Hydrogen
    • Example: 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂
  • Reaction with acids: Metals react with acids to form metal salts and hydrogen gas
    • Metal + Acid → Metal salt + Hydrogen
    • Example: Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂
  • Displacement reactions: More reactive metals can displace less reactive metals from their salt solutions

Chemical Properties of Non-metals

  • Reaction with oxygen: Non-metals form acidic or neutral oxides
    • Non-metal + Oxygen → Non-metal oxide
    • Example: C + O₂ → CO₂
  • Reaction with water: Most non-metals do not react with water
  • Reaction with acids: Non-metals generally do not react with acids
  • Reaction with hydrogen: Some non-metals react with hydrogen to form covalent hydrides
    • Example: H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl

Activity Series of Metals

  • The arrangement of metals in order of their decreasing reactivity is called the activity series
  • Order (most reactive to least reactive): K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Pb > H > Cu > Hg > Ag > Au
  • Metals above hydrogen can displace hydrogen from acids
  • More reactive metals can displace less reactive metals from their salt solutions
  • Metals at the top are more easily oxidized and harder to reduce

Occurrence of Metals in Nature

  • Minerals: Naturally occurring compounds of metals found in the earth's crust
  • Ores: Minerals from which metals can be extracted profitably
  • Gangue: Unwanted materials present in ores
  • Native state: Some unreactive metals like gold, silver, and platinum occur in free state
  • Combined state: Most metals occur as compounds (oxides, sulfides, carbonates, etc.)

Extraction of Metals

  • The process of obtaining metals from their ores is called metallurgy
  • Concentration of ore: Removal of unwanted materials (gangue) from ore
  • Reduction: Conversion of metal compounds to free metals
    • By heating with carbon: For moderately reactive metals (Zn, Fe, Pb)
    • By electrolysis: For highly reactive metals (Na, Ca, Mg, Al)
    • By heating alone: For low reactive metals (Hg, Ag)
  • Refining: Purification of crude metals obtained from reduction

Corrosion

  • The process of slow eating up of metals due to attack by oxygen, water, acids, etc., is called corrosion
  • Rusting of iron: Formation of reddish-brown iron oxide (Fe₂O₃.xH₂O) on iron surfaces
  • Conditions for rusting: Both oxygen and water are necessary
  • Prevention of corrosion:
    • Painting or oiling
    • Galvanization (coating with zinc)
    • Electroplating
    • Alloying

Alloys

  • Alloys are homogeneous mixtures of two or more metals or a metal and a non-metal
  • Properties of alloys are different from their constituent metals
  • Common alloys:
    • Steel: Iron + Carbon (0.1-1.5%)
    • Stainless steel: Iron + Chromium + Nickel
    • Brass: Copper + Zinc
    • Bronze: Copper + Tin
    • Amalgam: Mercury + other metals
  • Advantages: Greater strength, resistance to corrosion, desired properties

Important Questions and Solutions

Question 1

Write the balanced chemical equations for the following reactions:

(a) Zinc reacts with dilute hydrochloric acid

(b) Iron reacts with steam

(c) Sodium reacts with water

Solution:

(a) Zinc reacts with dilute hydrochloric acid:

Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑

Zinc displaces hydrogen from hydrochloric acid to form zinc chloride and hydrogen gas is evolved.

(b) Iron reacts with steam:

3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂↑

Iron reacts with steam to form iron oxide (Fe₃O₄) and hydrogen gas.

(c) Sodium reacts with water:

2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂↑

Sodium reacts vigorously with water to form sodium hydroxide and hydrogen gas. The reaction is highly exothermic.

Question 2

What is the activity series of metals? Arrange the following metals in increasing order of their reactivity: Iron, Copper, Zinc, Aluminum.

Solution:

Activity Series of Metals:

The activity series is the arrangement of metals in order of their decreasing reactivity. It helps predict whether a metal can displace another metal from its salt solution or acid.

Complete Activity Series (most reactive to least reactive):

K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Pb > H > Cu > Hg > Ag > Au

Given metals in increasing order of reactivity:

Copper < Iron < Zinc < Aluminum

Cu < Fe < Zn < Al

This means aluminum is most reactive among the given metals, followed by zinc, then iron, and copper is the least reactive.

Question 3

Explain the extraction of aluminum from bauxite ore. Why is aluminum extracted by electrolysis and not by reduction with carbon?

Solution:

Extraction of Aluminum from Bauxite:

Step 1: Purification of Bauxite (Bayer's Process)

Bauxite ore contains impurities like iron oxide and silica. It is treated with concentrated NaOH solution at high temperature and pressure to form sodium aluminate.

Al₂O₃ + 2NaOH + 3H₂O → 2Na[Al(OH)₄]

Step 2: Precipitation

The solution is diluted and cooled, causing aluminum hydroxide to precipitate.

Na[Al(OH)₄] → Al(OH)₃ + NaOH

Step 3: Calcination

Aluminum hydroxide is heated strongly to get pure aluminum oxide.

2Al(OH)₃ → Al₂O₃ + 3H₂O

Step 4: Electrolysis (Hall-Heroult Process)

Pure Al₂O₃ is mixed with cryolite (Na₃AlF₆) and electrolyzed using carbon electrodes.

At cathode: Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al

At anode: 2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻

Why electrolysis instead of carbon reduction?

Aluminum is highly reactive and has strong affinity for oxygen. Carbon cannot reduce aluminum oxide because aluminum is more reactive than carbon. The high temperature required would make the process uneconomical. Therefore, electrolysis is used despite being energy-intensive.

Key Differences

Property Metals Non-metals
Appearance Lustrous (shiny) Dull (except iodine, graphite)
Malleability Malleable Brittle
Ductility Ductile Non-ductile
Conductivity Good conductors Poor conductors (except graphite)
Density High density Low density
Melting/Boiling Point Generally high Generally low
Nature of Oxides Basic oxides Acidic or neutral oxides
Electron Transfer Lose electrons (reducing agents) Gain electrons (oxidizing agents)

Common Misconceptions

All Metals are Hard

Misconception: All metals are hard and have high melting points.

Correction: Alkali metals like sodium and potassium are soft and can be cut with a knife. Mercury is liquid at room temperature.

All Non-metals are Gases

Misconception: All non-metals exist as gases at room temperature.

Correction: Non-metals exist in all three states - solids (carbon, sulfur, phosphorus), liquids (bromine), and gases (oxygen, nitrogen, chlorine).

Graphite is a Metal

Misconception: Since graphite conducts electricity, it must be a metal.

Correction: Graphite is an allotrope of carbon (a non-metal) that conducts electricity due to the presence of free electrons in its structure. Electrical conductivity alone doesn't determine if a substance is metallic.

NCERT Exercise Solutions

Exercise Question 1

Give reasons why copper is used to make hot water tanks and not steel (an alloy of iron).

Solution:

Copper is used to make hot water tanks instead of steel for the following reasons:

  1. Corrosion Resistance: Copper does not react with water even at high temperatures, while iron (steel) rusts when it comes in contact with water and oxygen.
  2. Good Heat Conductor: Copper is an excellent conductor of heat, allowing efficient heat transfer from the heating element to water.
  3. Durability: Copper tanks last longer due to their resistance to corrosion and chemical reactions.
  4. Non-toxic: Copper does not release harmful substances into water, making it safe for storing drinking water.
  5. Antimicrobial Properties: Copper has natural antimicrobial properties that help prevent bacterial growth.

Steel, being an alloy of iron, would rust and contaminate the water, making it unsuitable for hot water tanks.

Exercise Question 2

An element A is soft and can be cut with a knife. This is very reactive to air and cannot be kept open in air. It reacts vigorously with water. Identify the element from the following:

a) Mg b) Na c) P d) Ca

Solution:

Answer: b) Na (Sodium)

Reasoning:

The given properties match sodium (Na):

  • Soft and can be cut with knife: Sodium is an alkali metal that is soft enough to be cut with a knife due to weak metallic bonding.
  • Very reactive to air: Sodium reacts rapidly with oxygen in air to form sodium oxide, so it must be stored under kerosene.
  • Cannot be kept open in air: Due to its high reactivity with oxygen and moisture in air.
  • Reacts vigorously with water: Sodium reacts violently with water, producing sodium hydroxide and hydrogen gas with release of heat.

Chemical equation for reaction with water:

2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂↑ + Heat

Other options don't fit all the criteria: Mg and Ca are harder metals, and P (phosphorus) is a non-metal.
