Chapter 15: Our Environment

Class 10 Science Updated: May 15, 2025

Introduction

Our Environment is a vital chapter that explores the delicate balance of ecosystems, environmental concerns, and the impact of human activities on nature. This chapter helps students understand the importance of environmental conservation and sustainable practices.

Key Concepts

Ecosystem

  • Components:
    • Biotic Components (Living):
      • Producers (Green plants)
      • Consumers (Herbivores, Carnivores, Omnivores)
      • Decomposers (Bacteria, Fungi)
    • Abiotic Components (Non-living):
      • Air
      • Water
      • Soil
      • Sunlight
      • Temperature
  • Interactions between components maintain balance
  • Energy flow through food chains and food webs

Food Chain and Food Web

  • Food Chain:
    • Linear sequence of eating and being eaten
    • Starts with producers
    • Energy flows from one trophic level to next
    • Only 10% energy transfer between levels
  • Food Web:
    • Network of interconnected food chains
    • More realistic representation of ecosystem
    • Shows multiple feeding relationships
    • Demonstrates ecosystem stability
  • Trophic Levels:
    • T1: Producers (Green plants)
    • T2: Primary consumers (Herbivores)
    • T3: Secondary consumers (Carnivores)
    • T4: Tertiary consumers (Top carnivores)

Environmental Problems

  • Ozone Depletion:
    • Caused by CFCs and other chemicals
    • Leads to increased UV radiation
    • Health risks and environmental damage
  • Waste Management:
    • Types of waste:
      • Biodegradable waste
      • Non-biodegradable waste
    • Problems:
      • Soil pollution
      • Water pollution
      • Air pollution

Biodegradable and Non-biodegradable Substances

  • Biodegradable:
    • Can be broken down by natural processes
    • Examples: Food waste, paper, wood
    • Decomposed by microorganisms
  • Non-biodegradable:
    • Cannot be broken down naturally
    • Examples: Plastics, metals, glass
    • Persist in environment for long periods
    • Cause pollution and harm to organisms

Important Questions and Solutions

Question 1

What is a food chain? Why is the flow of energy in a food chain unidirectional? Explain with an example.

Solution:

Food Chain:

A food chain is a linear sequence of organisms where each organism serves as a source of food for the next organism in the sequence.

Unidirectional Energy Flow:

  • Energy flows from producers to consumers
  • Only 10% energy transfers to next level
  • Rest is lost as heat, respiration, etc.
  • Energy cannot flow backwards

Example:

Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle

  • Grass (Producer) captures 1000 joules of solar energy
  • Grasshopper (Primary Consumer) gets 100 joules
  • Frog (Secondary Consumer) gets 10 joules
  • Snake (Tertiary Consumer) gets 1 joule
  • Eagle (Top Consumer) gets 0.1 joules

Question 2

Differentiate between biodegradable and non-biodegradable substances with examples. What are their environmental impacts?

Solution:

Aspect Biodegradable Non-biodegradable
Definition Can be decomposed by natural processes Cannot be decomposed naturally
Examples Food waste, paper, leaves Plastics, metals, glass
Time for Decomposition Days to months Years to centuries
Environmental Impact Minimal, enriches soil Pollution, harm to wildlife

Question 3

What is an ecosystem? Describe the various components of an ecosystem with examples.

Solution:

Ecosystem: A system where living organisms interact among themselves and with their physical environment.

Components:

  1. Biotic Components:
    • Producers: Green plants, algae
    • Consumers:
      • Primary: Deer, rabbit
      • Secondary: Fox, snake
      • Tertiary: Lion, eagle
    • Decomposers: Bacteria, fungi
  2. Abiotic Components:
    • Physical factors:
      • Air (oxygen, carbon dioxide)
      • Water
      • Soil
      • Temperature
    • Chemical factors:
      • pH
      • Minerals
      • Salinity

Key Points to Remember

Energy Flow in Food Chain

- Only 10% energy transfers to next level
- Decreasing energy at each trophic level
- Unidirectional flow

Ecosystem Balance

- Producers = Primary energy capturers
- Consumers = Energy transferers
- Decomposers = Nutrient recyclers

Environmental Protection

- Reduce waste generation
- Recycle non-biodegradable waste
- Proper waste disposal
- Avoid harmful chemicals

Common Misconceptions

Food Chain Complexity

Misconception: Food chains are simple, linear relationships.

Correction: In reality, food chains are interconnected forming complex food webs with multiple feeding relationships.

Decomposition Process

Misconception: All waste eventually decomposes.

Correction: Non-biodegradable substances can persist for hundreds of years without decomposing.

Ecosystem Stability

Misconception: Ecosystems are always stable and self-regulating.

Correction: Ecosystems are sensitive to changes and can be disrupted by human activities or natural disasters.
