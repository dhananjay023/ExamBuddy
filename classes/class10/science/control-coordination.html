Chapter 7: Control and Coordination

Class 10 Science Updated: May 15, 2025

Introduction

Control and Coordination is a crucial chapter in Class 10 Science that explores how living organisms respond to their environment and coordinate various life processes. This chapter covers the nervous system, hormonal system in animals, and coordination in plants through various mechanisms.

Key Concepts

Nervous System in Animals

  • The nervous system is made up of specialized cells called neurons
  • Neurons have three main parts: cell body (cyton), dendrites, and axon
  • Nerve impulses are electrical signals that travel along neurons
  • Synapse is the junction between two neurons where nerve impulses pass
  • Central Nervous System (CNS) includes brain and spinal cord
  • Peripheral Nervous System (PNS) includes all nerves outside CNS
  • Brain has three main parts: forebrain, midbrain, and hindbrain
  • Spinal cord acts as a pathway for nerve impulses and controls reflex actions

Reflex Actions

  • Reflex actions are automatic responses to stimuli that don't involve conscious thought
  • They help protect the body from harm and occur very quickly
  • Reflex arc is the pathway taken by nerve impulses during a reflex action
  • Components of reflex arc: receptor → sensory neuron → spinal cord → motor neuron → effector
  • Examples include withdrawing hand from hot objects, knee-jerk reflex, blinking
  • Reflex actions are controlled by the spinal cord, not the brain
  • This allows for faster response times in emergency situations

Human Brain

  • Forebrain: Largest part containing cerebrum, responsible for thinking, memory, and voluntary actions
  • Cerebrum is divided into left and right hemispheres
  • Different regions of cerebrum control different functions (motor, sensory, speech areas)
  • Midbrain: Controls eye movement and reflex actions involving hearing and sight
  • Hindbrain: Includes cerebellum, pons, and medulla oblongata
  • Cerebellum: Controls balance, posture, and coordination of voluntary movements
  • Medulla oblongata: Controls involuntary actions like breathing, heartbeat, and blood pressure
  • Brain is protected by skull and cerebrospinal fluid

Hormonal System (Endocrine System)

  • Hormones are chemical messengers produced by endocrine glands
  • They coordinate and control metabolic processes in the body
  • Hormones are secreted directly into the bloodstream (ductless glands)
  • Major endocrine glands: pituitary, thyroid, adrenal, pancreas, gonads
  • Pituitary gland: Called "master gland," controls other endocrine glands
  • Thyroid gland: Produces thyroxine, controls metabolism and growth
  • Adrenal glands: Produce adrenaline for "fight or flight" response
  • Pancreas: Produces insulin to regulate blood sugar levels
  • Gonads: Produce sex hormones (testosterone in males, estrogen and progesterone in females)

Coordination in Plants

  • Plants don't have a nervous system but show coordination through movements
  • Plant movements are generally slow and in response to stimuli
  • Tropisms: Directional growth movements in response to stimuli
  • Phototropism: Movement in response to light (shoots grow toward light)
  • Geotropism/Gravitropism: Movement in response to gravity (roots grow downward)
  • Hydrotropism: Movement in response to water
  • Chemotropism: Movement in response to chemicals
  • Thigmotropism: Movement in response to touch (tendrils coiling around support)
  • Nastic movements: Non-directional movements (like folding of leaves in Mimosa)
  • Plant hormones (auxins, gibberellins, cytokinins) control growth and development

Important Questions and Solutions

Question 1

What is a reflex action? Describe the pathway of a reflex arc with the help of a flow chart.

Solution:

A reflex action is an automatic, involuntary response to a stimulus that occurs without conscious thought. It helps protect the body from harmful situations and occurs very rapidly.

Characteristics of reflex actions:

  • Automatic and involuntary
  • Very fast response
  • Protective in nature
  • Controlled by spinal cord, not brain

Pathway of Reflex Arc:

STIMULUS

↓

RECEPTOR (Sensory organ)

↓

SENSORY NEURON

↓

SPINAL CORD (Central Nervous System)

↓

MOTOR NEURON

↓

EFFECTOR (Muscle/Gland)

↓

RESPONSE

Example: When you touch a hot object, sensory receptors in your skin detect heat, send signals through sensory neurons to the spinal cord, which immediately sends signals through motor neurons to muscles, causing you to withdraw your hand.

Solution:

Mode of transmission Through neurons Through bloodstream

Question 3

What are plant hormones? Name the plant hormones and write their functions.

Solution:

Plant hormones (also called phytohormones) are naturally occurring chemical compounds that regulate various physiological processes in plants such as growth, development, reproduction, and responses to environmental stimuli.

Major Plant Hormones and their Functions:

  1. Auxins:
    • Promote cell elongation and growth
    • Control apical dominance
    • Responsible for phototropism and geotropism
    • Help in formation of roots
    • Prevent leaf and fruit drop
  2. Gibberellins:
    • Promote stem elongation
    • Help in seed germination
    • Stimulate flowering in some plants
    • Promote fruit development
    • Break seed and bud dormancy
  3. Cytokinins:
    • Promote cell division
    • Delay senescence (aging) of leaves
    • Help in breaking apical dominance
    • Promote growth of lateral buds
    • Involved in root and shoot development
  4. Abscisic Acid (ABA):
    • Inhibits growth and promotes dormancy
    • Promotes closure of stomata during water stress
    • Prevents germination of seeds
    • Helps plants cope with stress conditions

Common Misconceptions

Brain Controls All Actions

Misconception: The brain controls all actions in the body.

Correction: While the brain controls voluntary actions and higher-order thinking, reflex actions are controlled by the spinal cord without brain involvement for faster response.

Plants Don't Respond to Stimuli

Misconception: Plants cannot respond to environmental stimuli because they don't have a nervous system.

Correction: Plants respond to various stimuli through tropisms and nastic movements, coordinated by plant hormones rather than a nervous system.

Hormones Work Instantly

Misconception: Hormones work as quickly as nerve impulses.

Correction: Hormonal responses are generally slower than nervous responses but have longer-lasting effects. They travel through the bloodstream, which takes time.

Exercise Question 1

How does phototropism occur in plants?

Solution:

Phototropism is the growth movement of plants in response to light stimulus. It occurs through the following mechanism:

  1. Light detection: The tip of the plant shoot (coleoptile) detects the direction of light source.
  2. Auxin distribution: When light comes from one side, auxin hormone accumulates on the shaded side of the shoot tip.
  3. Differential growth: Higher concentration of auxin on the shaded side causes cells to elongate more on that side compared to the illuminated side.
  4. Bending movement: Due to unequal cell elongation, the shoot bends toward the light source.
  5. Adaptive significance: This movement helps the plant orient its photosynthetic parts (leaves) toward light for maximum photosynthesis.

Experimental evidence: Darwin's experiments with canary grass showed that when the tip is covered or removed, phototropism doesn't occur, proving that the tip is essential for detecting light and producing the bending response.

Types of phototropism:

  • Positive phototropism: Growth toward light (shoots)
  • Negative phototropism: Growth away from light (roots)
