Chapter 12: Electricity

Class 10 Science Updated: May 15, 2025

Introduction

Electricity is one of the most important chapters in Class 10 Science. It introduces fundamental concepts about electric current, potential difference, resistance, and electrical circuits. This chapter forms the foundation for many advanced topics in physics.

Key Concepts

Electric Current and Circuit

  • Electric current is the flow of electric charge through a conductor
  • The SI unit of electric current is ampere (A)
  • 1 ampere is defined as the flow of 1 coulomb of charge per second
  • Conventional current flows from positive to negative terminal
  • Actual electron flow is from negative to positive terminal
  • A closed conducting loop with a source of electricity is called an electric circuit

Potential Difference and Voltage

  • Potential difference is the work done to move a unit charge from one point to another
  • The SI unit of potential difference is volt (V)
  • 1 volt is defined as 1 joule of work done per coulomb of charge
  • Potential difference is necessary for current to flow
  • Batteries and cells create potential difference through chemical reactions

Resistance and Resistivity

  • Resistance is the property of a conductor that opposes the flow of current
  • The SI unit of resistance is ohm (Ω)
  • 1 ohm is the resistance of a conductor when 1 ampere of current flows through it at a potential difference of 1 volt
  • Resistivity (ρ) is the characteristic property of the material
  • Resistance (R) of a uniform conductor is directly proportional to its length (l) and inversely proportional to its area of cross-section (A)
  • Formula: R = ρ × l/A, where ρ is the resistivity
  • Resistivity depends on the nature of the material and temperature
  • The resistivity of metals increases with temperature, while that of semiconductors and insulators decreases with temperature

Ohm's Law

  • Ohm's law states that the current through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference applied across it, provided the temperature and other physical conditions remain constant
  • Mathematically expressed as V = IR, where V is the potential difference, I is the current, and R is the resistance
  • The graph of V vs. I is a straight line for ohmic conductors
  • Materials that follow Ohm's law are called ohmic conductors (e.g., metals)
  • Materials that do not follow Ohm's law are called non-ohmic conductors (e.g., diodes, thermistors)

Ohm's Law

V = I × R

Where:

  • V = Potential difference in volts (V)
  • I = Current in amperes (A)
  • R = Resistance in ohms (Ω)

Combination of Resistors

  • Resistors can be combined in series, parallel, or mixed arrangements
  • In a series combination:
    • The same current flows through each resistor
    • The total resistance Rs = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...
    • The total resistance is greater than the highest individual resistance
  • In a parallel combination:
    • The same potential difference exists across each resistor
    • The total resistance Rp is calculated as: 1/Rp = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...
    • The total resistance is less than the lowest individual resistance

Series Combination

Rs = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...

Parallel Combination

1/Rp = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...

Heating Effect of Electric Current

  • When electric current passes through a resistor, heat is produced due to the conversion of electrical energy to heat energy
  • This is known as Joule's heating or Joule's effect
  • The heat produced (H) is given by Joule's law of heating: H = I²Rt, where:
    • I is the current
    • R is the resistance
    • t is the time for which current flows
  • Applications of heating effect include electric iron, electric heater, electric bulb, electric fuse, etc.

Joule's Law of Heating

H = I² × R × t

Where:

  • H = Heat produced in joules (J)
  • I = Current in amperes (A)
  • R = Resistance in ohms (Ω)
  • t = Time in seconds (s)

Electric Power

  • Electric power is the rate at which electrical energy is consumed or converted into other forms of energy
  • The SI unit of electric power is watt (W)
  • 1 watt is the power consumed when 1 ampere of current flows through a potential difference of 1 volt
  • P = VI, where P is power, V is potential difference, and I is current
  • Using Ohm's law (V = IR), we can express power as:
    • P = VI
    • P = I²R
    • P = V²/R
  • The commercial unit of electrical energy is kilowatt-hour (kWh)
  • 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J

Electric Power

P = V × I = I² × R = V²/R

Where:

  • P = Power in watts (W)
  • V = Potential difference in volts (V)
  • I = Current in amperes (A)
  • R = Resistance in ohms (Ω)

Important Questions and Solutions

Question 1

State Ohm's law. How is it verified experimentally?

Solution:

Ohm's law states that the current flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference applied across its ends, provided the temperature and other physical conditions remain constant.

Mathematically, V = IR, where V is the potential difference, I is the current, and R is the resistance.

Experimental verification of Ohm's law:

  1. Set up a circuit with a battery, ammeter, voltmeter, resistor, and rheostat connected in series.
  2. Vary the resistance using the rheostat to change the current in the circuit.
  3. For each setting, record the readings of current (I) from the ammeter and voltage (V) from the voltmeter.
  4. Plot a graph of voltage (V) versus current (I).
  5. If the graph is a straight line passing through the origin, Ohm's law is verified.
  6. The slope of this line gives the resistance (R) of the conductor.

Question 2

Three resistors of 5 Ω, 10 Ω, and 15 Ω are connected in (a) series, and (b) parallel. Calculate the equivalent resistance in each case.

Solution:

(a) Series combination:

When resistors are connected in series, the equivalent resistance is the sum of individual resistances.

Rs = R₁ + R₂ + R₃

Rs = 5 Ω + 10 Ω + 15 Ω = 30 Ω

Therefore, the equivalent resistance in series connection is 30 Ω.

(b) Parallel combination:

When resistors are connected in parallel, the reciprocal of the equivalent resistance is the sum of the reciprocals of individual resistances.

1/Rp = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃

1/Rp = 1/5 Ω + 1/10 Ω + 1/15 Ω

1/Rp = 0.2 + 0.1 + 0.067 = 0.367

Rp = 1/0.367 = 2.72 Ω

Therefore, the equivalent resistance in parallel connection is 2.72 Ω.

Question 3

An electric iron consumes 1.5 kW of power when operated at 220 V. Calculate:

(a) The current through the iron

(b) The resistance of the iron

(c) The energy consumed in 2 hours

Solution:

Given:

Power (P) = 1.5 kW = 1500 W

Voltage (V) = 220 V

Time (t) = 2 hours

(a) Current through the iron:

Using the formula P = VI, we can calculate the current as I = P/V

I = 1500 W / 220 V = 6.82 A

(b) Resistance of the iron:

Using Ohm's law, R = V/I

R = 220 V / 6.82 A = 32.26 Ω

Alternatively, using P = V²/R, we get R = V²/P

R = (220 V)² / 1500 W = 48400 / 1500 = 32.27 Ω

(c) Energy consumed in 2 hours:

Energy = Power × Time

Energy = 1.5 kW × 2 h = 3 kWh

Therefore, the energy consumed in 2 hours is 3 kilowatt-hours.

Common Misconceptions

Current vs. Voltage

Misconception: Current and voltage are the same thing.

Correction: Current is the flow of electric charge, while voltage (potential difference) is the energy difference that causes the charges to flow. They are related but distinct quantities.

Current "Consumption"

Misconception: Current is "used up" as it flows through a circuit.

Correction: Current is conserved in a circuit. The same amount of current that enters a component also exits it. What is "used up" is the electrical energy, which is converted to other forms such as heat, light, or sound.

Higher Resistance Always Means Lower Current

Misconception: Adding more resistance to a circuit always reduces the current.

Correction: While this is true for resistors in series, adding resistors in parallel actually decreases the total resistance and increases the current from the source.

NCERT Exercise Solutions

Below are the solutions to selected questions from the NCERT exercise.

Exercise Question 1

A piece of wire of resistance 20 Ω is drawn out so that its length is increased to twice its original length. Calculate the resistance of the wire in the new situation.

Solution:

Given:

Original resistance (R₁) = 20 Ω

Length is doubled: new length (l₂) = 2 × original length (l₁)

We know that resistance (R) of a wire is directly proportional to its length (l) and inversely proportional to its area of cross-section (A):

R = ρ × l/A, where ρ is the resistivity of the material

When a wire is drawn, its volume remains constant. If length increases by a factor of 2, the area of cross-section decreases by the same factor.

So, if l₂ = 2l₁, then A₂ = A₁/2

For new resistance R₂:

R₂ = ρ × l₂/A₂ = ρ × (2l₁)/(A₁/2) = 4 × ρ × l₁/A₁ = 4 × R₁

R₂ = 4 × 20 Ω = 80 Ω

Therefore, the new resistance of the wire is 80 Ω.
