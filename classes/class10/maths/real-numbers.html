Chapter 1: Real Numbers

Class 10 Mathematics Updated: May 15, 2025

Introduction

Real Numbers is the first chapter in Class 10 Mathematics. It builds upon your understanding of number systems from previous classes and introduces important concepts like Euclid's Division Lemma, the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic, and more. This chapter provides the foundation for various mathematical operations and proofs used throughout your mathematical journey.

Key Concepts

Euclid's Division Lemma

  • For any two positive integers a and b, there exist unique integers q and r such that a = bq + r, where 0 ≤ r < b
  • q is called the quotient
  • r is called the remainder
  • When r = 0, we say that b divides a, denoted as b | a
  • Applications include finding HCF using the division algorithm

Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

  • Every natural number greater than 1 can be expressed as a product of prime numbers
  • This factorization is unique except for the order of factors
  • For example: 60 = 2² × 3 × 5
  • Prime factorization helps in finding LCM and HCF
  • It also helps in determining whether a number is rational or irrational

HCF and LCM

  • HCF (Highest Common Factor) is the largest positive integer that divides each of the given numbers
  • LCM (Least Common Multiple) is the smallest positive integer that is divisible by each of the given numbers
  • For two numbers a and b: HCF(a, b) × LCM(a, b) = a × b
  • The HCF can be found using the Euclidean Algorithm, which is based on Euclid's Division Lemma
  • The LCM can be calculated using prime factorization

Euclidean Algorithm for HCF

To find HCF of two positive integers a and b (where a > b):

  1. Apply Euclid's Division Lemma to get a = bq + r
  2. If r = 0, then HCF(a, b) = b
  3. If r ≠ 0, then HCF(a, b) = HCF(b, r)
  4. Repeat the process with b and r until remainder becomes 0

Rational Numbers and Their Decimal Expansions

  • A rational number is a number that can be expressed as p/q, where p and q are integers and q ≠ 0
  • The decimal expansion of a rational number is either terminating or non-terminating recurring
  • A rational number p/q has a terminating decimal expansion if the prime factorization of q is of the form 2ᵐ × 5ⁿ, where m, n are non-negative integers
  • A rational number p/q has a non-terminating recurring decimal expansion if the prime factorization of q contains primes other than 2 and 5

Irrational Numbers

  • An irrational number cannot be expressed as p/q, where p and q are integers and q ≠ 0
  • The decimal expansion of an irrational number is non-terminating and non-recurring
  • Examples include √2, √3, π, and e
  • The sum and product of a rational and an irrational number is always irrational
  • The sum or product of two irrational numbers may be rational or irrational

Important Questions and Solutions

Question 1

Use Euclid's Division Lemma to show that the square of any positive integer is either of the form 3m or 3m + 1 for some integer m.

Solution:

Using Euclid's Division Lemma, when any positive integer a is divided by 3, the remainder r can be 0, 1, or 2.

So we have three cases:

Case 1: When a = 3q (remainder 0)

a² = (3q)² = 9q² = 3(3q²)

This is of the form 3m where m = 3q²

Case 2: When a = 3q + 1 (remainder 1)

a² = (3q + 1)² = 9q² + 6q + 1 = 3(3q² + 2q) + 1

This is of the form 3m + 1 where m = 3q² + 2q

Case 3: When a = 3q + 2 (remainder 2)

a² = (3q + 2)² = 9q² + 12q + 4 = 9q² + 12q + 3 + 1 = 3(3q² + 4q + 1) + 1

This is also of the form 3m + 1 where m = 3q² + 4q + 1

Therefore, the square of any positive integer is either of the form 3m or 3m + 1 for some integer m.

Question 2

Find the HCF of 96 and 404 by applying the Euclidean Algorithm.

Solution:

We apply the Euclidean Algorithm to find the HCF of 96 and 404:

Step 1: 404 = 96 × 4 + 20

Step 2: 96 = 20 × 4 + 16

Step 3: 20 = 16 × 1 + 4

Step 4: 16 = 4 × 4 + 0

Since the remainder becomes 0, the HCF is the last non-zero remainder, which is 4.

Therefore, HCF(96, 404) = 4

Question 3

Show that √5 is irrational.

Solution:

We will use proof by contradiction to show that √5 is irrational.

Let's assume that √5 is rational. Then √5 can be expressed as p/q, where p and q are coprime positive integers (i.e., their HCF is 1).

So, √5 = p/q

Squaring both sides: 5 = p²/q²

Multiplying both sides by q²: 5q² = p²

This means p² is divisible by 5, which implies that p is divisible by 5 (since if a prime divides a square, it also divides the number).

So p = 5k for some integer k.

Substituting: 5q² = (5k)² = 25k²

Dividing both sides by 5: q² = 5k²

This means q² is divisible by 5, which implies that q is also divisible by 5.

But this contradicts our assumption that p and q are coprime (have no common factors other than 1).

Therefore, our initial assumption that √5 is rational must be false, which means √5 is irrational.

Key Formulas

Euclid's Division Lemma

a = bq + r, where 0 ≤ r < b

HCF and LCM Relation

HCF(a, b) × LCM(a, b) = a × b

Division Algorithm

HCF(a, b) = HCF(b, r) where r is remainder when a is divided by b

Common Misconceptions

All Decimal Numbers are Rational

Misconception: All decimal numbers are rational numbers.

Correction: Decimal numbers can be rational or irrational. A decimal number is rational if and only if its decimal expansion is either terminating or non-terminating recurring. Numbers like π and √2 have non-terminating, non-recurring decimal expansions and are irrational.

HCF is Always a Small Number

Misconception: The HCF of two numbers is always a small number.

Correction: The HCF can be as large as the smaller of the two numbers if one number divides the other. For example, HCF(8, 24) = 8.

Prime Factorization is Unique Only for Prime Numbers

Misconception: Only prime numbers have a unique prime factorization.

Correction: According to the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic, every integer greater than 1 (not just prime numbers) has a unique prime factorization, except for the order of factors.

Exercise 1.1 Question 1

Use Euclid's division algorithm to find the HCF of:

(i) 135 and 225

(ii) 196 and 38220

(iii) 867 and 255

Solution:

(i) HCF of 135 and 225

Step 1: 225 = 135 × 1 + 90

Step 2: 135 = 90 × 1 + 45

Step 3: 90 = 45 × 2 + 0

Since the remainder is 0, the HCF is 45.

Therefore, HCF(135, 225) = 45

(ii) HCF of 196 and 38220

Step 1: 38220 = 196 × 195 + 0

Since the remainder is 0, the HCF is 196.

Therefore, HCF(196, 38220) = 196

(iii) HCF of 867 and 255

Step 1: 867 = 255 × 3 + 102

Step 2: 255 = 102 × 2 + 51

Step 3: 102 = 51 × 2 + 0

Since the remainder is 0, the HCF is 51.

Therefore, HCF(867, 255) = 51
