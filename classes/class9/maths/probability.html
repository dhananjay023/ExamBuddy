Chapter 15: Probability

Class 9 Mathematics Updated: May 15, 2025

Introduction

Probability is the study of chance and uncertainty. This chapter introduces the basic concepts of probability, helping students understand how to calculate the likelihood of various events occurring. From simple coin tosses to complex real-world scenarios, probability theory provides tools to make predictions and informed decisions in situations involving uncertainty.

Key Concepts

Basic Terms

  • Random Experiment:
    • Well-defined outcomes
    • Unpredictable results
    • Can be repeated
  • Sample Space:
    • Set of all possible outcomes
    • Denoted by S or Ω
    • Examples: {H,T}, {1,2,3,4,5,6}
  • Event:
    • Subset of sample space
    • Simple events
    • Compound events

Probability Calculation

  • Classical Definition:
    • P(E) = n(E)/n(S)
    • n(E) = favorable outcomes
    • n(S) = total outcomes
  • Properties:
    • 0 ≤ P(E) ≤ 1
    • P(S) = 1
    • P(impossible event) = 0

Types of Events

  • Mutually Exclusive:
    • Cannot occur together
    • P(A∩B) = 0
    • P(A∪B) = P(A) + P(B)
  • Complementary Events:
    • P(E) + P(E') = 1
    • Cover all possibilities
    • No common elements

Applications

  • Real-world Examples:
    • Weather forecasting
    • Games of chance
    • Risk assessment
    • Insurance calculations
  • Decision Making:
    • Expected outcomes
    • Risk analysis
    • Strategic planning

Important Questions and Solutions

Question 1

A die is thrown once. Find the probability of getting:

a) A prime number
b) A number greater than 4

Solution:

  1. Sample space S = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}
  2. a) Prime numbers on die = {2, 3, 5}
    • P(prime) = n(E)/n(S) = 3/6 = 1/2
  3. b) Numbers greater than 4 = {5, 6}
    • P(>4) = n(E)/n(S) = 2/6 = 1/3

Question 2

Two coins are tossed simultaneously. Find the probability of getting:

a) At least one head
b) Exactly one head

Solution:

  1. Sample space S = {HH, HT, TH, TT}
  2. a) At least one head = {HH, HT, TH}
    • P(at least one H) = 3/4
  3. b) Exactly one head = {HT, TH}
    • P(exactly one H) = 2/4 = 1/2

Key Formulas

Basic Probability

P(E) = n(E)/n(S)

Complementary Events

P(E') = 1 - P(E)

Mutually Exclusive

P(A∪B) = P(A) + P(B)

Not Mutually Exclusive

P(A∪B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A∩B)

Common Misconceptions

Independence vs Mutual Exclusivity

Misconception: Independent events are mutually exclusive.

Correction: Independent events can occur together, while mutually exclusive events cannot.

Gambler's Fallacy

Misconception: Previous outcomes affect future probabilities in independent events.

Correction: Each independent trial has the same probability regardless of previous outcomes.

Equal Probability

Misconception: All outcomes are equally likely.

Correction: Outcomes may have different probabilities based on the nature of the experiment.
