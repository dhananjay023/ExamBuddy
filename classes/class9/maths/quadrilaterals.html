Chapter 8: Quadrilaterals

Class 9 Mathematics Updated: May 15, 2025

Introduction

A quadrilateral is a four-sided polygon. This chapter explores different types of quadrilaterals, their properties, and relationships between their angles and sides. Understanding quadrilaterals is essential for both theoretical geometry and real-world applications.

Key Concepts

Types of Quadrilaterals

  • Square:
    • All sides equal
    • All angles 90°
    • Diagonals equal and bisect at right angles
  • Rectangle:
    • Opposite sides equal
    • All angles 90°
    • Diagonals equal and bisect each other
  • Rhombus:
    • All sides equal
    • Opposite angles equal
    • Diagonals bisect at right angles
  • Parallelogram:
    • Opposite sides parallel and equal
    • Opposite angles equal
    • Diagonals bisect each other
  • Trapezium:
    • One pair of parallel sides
    • Non-parallel sides can be equal (isosceles)

Properties of Quadrilaterals

  • Sum of angles = 360°
  • Sum of exterior angles = 360°
  • Each exterior angle = 90° + adjacent interior angle
  • Diagonals divide quadrilateral into triangles
  • Area can be calculated using various formulas

Special Properties

  • Cyclic quadrilateral:
    • All vertices lie on a circle
    • Opposite angles are supplementary
    • Product of diagonals = sum of products of opposite sides
  • Kite:
    • Two pairs of adjacent sides equal
    • Diagonals perpendicular
    • One diagonal bisects the other

Important Questions and Solutions

Question 1

Prove that the diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other.

Solution:

  1. Given: ABCD is a parallelogram with diagonals AC and BD intersecting at O
  2. To prove: AO = OC and BO = OD
  3. Proof:
    • In triangles AOB and COD:
      • AB = CD (opposite sides of parallelogram)
      • ∠ABO = ∠CDO (alternate angles)
      • ∠BAO = ∠DCO (alternate angles)
    • By ASA congruence criterion, triangles are congruent
    • Therefore, AO = OC and BO = OD

Question 2

Show that if the diagonals of a quadrilateral bisect each other at right angles, then it is a rhombus.

Solution:

  1. Given: Diagonals AC and BD bisect each other at right angles at O
  2. To prove: ABCD is a rhombus
  3. Proof:
    • Since diagonals bisect: AO = OC and BO = OD
    • Since they intersect at right angles: ∠AOB = 90°
    • In triangles AOB and COD:
      • AO = OC (given)
      • BO = OD (given)
      • ∠AOB = ∠COD (vertically opposite angles)
    • By SAS congruence, triangles are congruent
    • Therefore, AB = BC = CD = DA
    • Hence, ABCD is a rhombus

Key Formulas

Angle Sum

∠A + ∠B + ∠C + ∠D = 360°

Area of Rectangle

A = l × b

Area of Square

A = a²

Area of Parallelogram

A = base × height

Common Misconceptions

Square vs Rectangle

Misconception: A square is not a rectangle.

Correction: A square is a special type of rectangle where all sides are equal.

Parallelogram Area

Misconception: The slant height is used to calculate area.

Correction: The perpendicular height (altitude) is used to calculate area.

Diagonal Properties

Misconception: All quadrilaterals with equal diagonals are rectangles.

Correction: Equal diagonals alone don't guarantee a rectangle - they must also bisect each other.
