Chapter 2: Is Matter Around Us Pure

Class 9 Science Updated: May 15, 2025

Introduction

This chapter explores the concept of pure substances and mixtures, their types, and various separation techniques. It helps students understand how matter exists in different forms and how we can separate different components of mixtures.

Key Concepts

Pure Substances

  • Matter made up of only one kind of particles
  • Elements are the simplest pure substances
  • Compounds are pure substances made of two or more elements
  • Have fixed composition and properties
  • Examples: Gold, water, sugar

Types of Mixtures

  • Homogeneous mixtures - uniform composition throughout
  • Heterogeneous mixtures - non-uniform composition
  • Solutions - homogeneous mixtures of solute and solvent
  • Colloids - heterogeneous mixtures with dispersed phase
  • Suspensions - heterogeneous mixtures that settle down

Separation Techniques

  • Filtration - separating insoluble solids from liquids
  • Distillation - separating miscible liquids
  • Crystallization - obtaining pure solids from solutions
  • Chromatography - separating different colored components
  • Sublimation - separating sublimable substances

Important Questions and Solutions

Question 1

Differentiate between homogeneous and heterogeneous mixtures with examples.

Solution:

Homogeneous Mixtures:

  • Have uniform composition throughout
  • Components cannot be seen separately
  • No visible boundaries between components
  • Examples: Sugar solution, air, brass

Heterogeneous Mixtures:

  • Have non-uniform composition
  • Components can be seen separately
  • Clear boundaries between components
  • Examples: Oil and water, sand and water, soil

Question 2

What is chromatography? Explain its principle and applications.

Solution:

Chromatography is a technique used to separate different components of a mixture based on their different solubilities in the same solvent.

Principle:

  • Different components have different rates of movement through a medium
  • Components separate based on their solubility and adsorption
  • More soluble components travel faster

Applications:

  • Separating pigments in plant extracts
  • Testing purity of substances
  • Separating dyes in inks
  • In forensic science for chemical analysis

Key Formulas and Calculations

Concentration Percentage

Concentration = (Mass of solute / Mass of solution) × 100

Solubility

Solubility = (Mass of solute / Mass of solvent) × 100

RF Value in Chromatography

RF = Distance moved by solute / Distance moved by solvent

Common Misconceptions

Pure Substances

Misconception: All pure substances are elements.

Correction: Pure substances can be either elements or compounds. Water is a pure substance but it's a compound, not an element.

Solutions

Misconception: All solutions are liquids.

Correction: Solutions can be solid (alloys), liquid (salt water), or gas (air) mixtures.

Filtration

Misconception: Filtration can separate all types of mixtures.

Correction: Filtration only works for heterogeneous mixtures with insoluble solid particles in liquids.
